If you scored at or above FUHSI's minimum JAMB benchmark and selected the Federal University of Health Sciences, Ila-Orangun (FUHSI) as your FIRST CHOICE institution, the next step in your admission journey is the FUHSI Screening Exercise. This guide explains exactly what the screening is, who qualifies, and how to prepare — without any of the confusing or invented "Post-UTME exam" claims that circulate online.

⚡ Quick Summary

FUHSI does not run a separate Post-UTME examination. Like most Nigerian universities, what FUHSI conducts is a screening exercise — the institution's own verification and ranking stage that takes place after JAMB. Some people loosely refer to this stage as "Post-UTME," but at FUHSI it is formally the Screening Exercise, and it is this screening that feeds into your final aggregate score.

What is the FUHSI Screening Exercise?

The Screening Exercise is the stage at which FUHSI verifies that candidates who chose the institution actually meet its requirements, and collects the O'Level and biodata information needed to compute an aggregate score for ranking. It is the university's own internal process — separate from, and conducted after, the JAMB UTME.

Your screening record, combined with your JAMB score and O'Level grades, is what FUHSI uses to calculate your aggregate score, which ultimately determines whether you are offered admission. For a full breakdown of how that aggregate is worked out, see our companion article, "How FUHSI Calculates Aggregate Scores."

Who Is Eligible for Screening?

To take part in the FUHSI Screening Exercise, you generally need to meet the following baseline requirements:

  • Sat the JAMB/UTME examination and scored at or above FUHSI's published minimum cut-off mark for the admission cycle
  • Selected FUHSI (Federal University of Health Sciences, Ila-Orangun) as your FIRST institution of choice on your JAMB registration
  • Have, or are expecting, the minimum number of O'Level credit passes (WAEC/NECO/GCE) required for your intended course, typically including English Language and Mathematics
  • Hold the relevant subject combination for your chosen programme (for example, Biology, Chemistry and Physics for most health-science courses)
⚠️ Important Notice

FUHSI's minimum JAMB cut-off, screening dates, and exact requirements can change from one admission cycle to the next. This article intentionally avoids quoting specific dates or cut-off figures so that it does not go out of date or mislead you. Always confirm current dates and requirements directly from the official FUHSI admissions office or the JAMB CAPS portal before taking any action.

How the Screening Process Generally Works

While exact steps can vary by admission cycle, the screening process at FUHSI typically follows this general pattern:

1

FUHSI Releases a Screening Notice

The university publishes an official notice — usually through its admissions office and JAMB CAPS — inviting candidates who meet the minimum cut-off to participate in screening.

2

Candidates Register / Submit Their Details

Eligible candidates complete the screening registration through the channel specified by FUHSI for that cycle, providing their JAMB details, O'Level results, and personal information.

3

Documents Are Verified

FUHSI checks the authenticity of submitted O'Level results, JAMB result slip, and other required documents against the candidate's claims.

4

Aggregate Score Is Computed

Using the verified JAMB score and O'Level grades, FUHSI calculates each candidate's aggregate score according to its admission formula for that year.

5

Candidates Are Ranked for Admission

Aggregate scores are used to rank candidates within each course, and admission offers are extended starting from the highest-ranked candidates, subject to available space.

Documents You Should Have Ready

Regardless of the exact screening format used in a given year, candidates are typically expected to have the following documents ready for verification:

  • JAMB result slip / registration printout for the relevant UTME year
  • O'Level result (WAEC/NECO/NABTEB — original certificate or verified result)
  • Birth certificate or statutory declaration of age
  • Local Government Identification or Certificate of Origin
  • Recent passport photographs
  • Any screening or acknowledgment slip issued by FUHSI for that cycle

Screening is primarily a verification and ranking exercise, not a second examination to "pass" or "fail." The most important thing a candidate can do is arrive with complete, accurate, and verifiable documents.

How to Prepare for FUHSI Screening

Because screening at FUHSI is centered on verification and aggregate computation rather than a separate timed test, the most useful preparation is administrative and informational rather than academic revision:

  • Gather your documents early. Confirm that your O'Level result is fully released and that names/dates match exactly across your JAMB profile, O'Level certificate, and birth records.
  • Know your own numbers. Work out your JAMB score and O'Level grade points in advance so you understand roughly where you stand before screening begins.
  • Watch the official channels. Screening notices are published by FUHSI and on the JAMB CAPS portal — check both regularly during the admission window rather than relying on rumors or unofficial sources.
  • Keep your JAMB profile updated. Make sure your phone number, email address, and O'Level result details on your JAMB profile are current, as these are often used to reach candidates.
  • Have a backup course in mind. If your aggregate may fall short for a highly competitive course, identify an alternative FUHSI programme where your numbers are more comfortably within range.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is FUHSI's screening the same as "Post-UTME"?

Not exactly. Many candidates use "Post-UTME" loosely to describe any post-JAMB university process. At FUHSI specifically, this stage is officially the Screening Exercise, and its main purpose is document verification and aggregate computation rather than a fresh subject-based examination.

Can I take part in screening if FUHSI was my second choice?

Generally, candidates who selected FUHSI as either their first o choice on JAMB are ELIGIBLE, provided they meet FUHSI's minimum cut-off and other published requirements. Always confirm the specific rule for the current admission cycle from the official FUHSI admissions office.

What if my O'Level result is still pending?

Requirements for "awaiting result" candidates vary by admission cycle and are determined solely by FUHSI and JAMB policy for that year. Confirm directly with the official portal whether this applies to you before assuming either way.

Conclusion

FUHSI's screening exercise is best understood as a verification and ranking stage, not a second examination. The strongest preparation is making sure your documents are complete and accurate, your JAMB profile is up to date, and you understand your own aggregate standing ahead of time. Use tools like MeritIQ to stay informed, and always verify dates and requirements from FUHSI's official channels before each admission cycle.

One of the most common questions among FUHSI aspirants is: "How exactly is my aggregate score calculated?" Based on historical screening data, FUHSI has used a small number of different aggregate formulas across recent admission cycles. This article breaks down each documented method, shows you how each one is calculated step-by-step, and applies all of them to one worked example so you can see how much the formula itself matters.

📐 The Short Answer

FUHSI's aggregate score combines your UTME (JAMB) score and your O'Level grades in weighted proportions. Historically, the split has been either 80:20 or 75:25 in favor of UTME, and the O'Level grading scale used inside that formula has also changed between cycles. Because the formula itself has changed more than once, the safest approach is to understand all documented methods rather than memorize a single one.

Three Documented Aggregate Methods

Based on available historical screening data, three aggregate calculation methods have been observed at FUHSI across different admission cycles:

Method 1 — 80:20 Formula Used ~3 admission cycles ago
80% UTME
20% O'Level

UTME component: your UTME score is divided by 400, then multiplied by 80%.

UTME Points = (UTME Score ÷ 400) × 80

O'Level component: your five core science/relevant subjects are each converted to points using this scale, then totalled, divided by the maximum possible (20), and multiplied by 20%.

A14 pts
B23.5 pts
B33 pts
C42 pts
C52.5 pts
C61 pt
O'Level Points = (Sum of 5 Grade Points ÷ 20) × 20

Final Aggregate = UTME Points + O'Level Points

Method 2 — 80:20 Formula (Updated Grading Scale) Used most recently
80% UTME
20% O'Level

FUHSI kept the same overall 80:20 weighting as Method 1, but changed the O'Level grading scale itself, giving stronger WAEC/NECO performance more relative value.

UTME Points = (UTME Score ÷ 400) × 80

Updated O'Level grading scale:

A16 pts
B25 pts
B34 pts
C43 pts
C52 pts
C61 pt

Because the grading scale changed, the maximum possible O'Level total becomes 30 (5 subjects × 6 points) instead of 20, so the divisor in the formula changes too.

O'Level Points = (Sum of 5 Grade Points ÷ 30) × 20

Final Aggregate = UTME Points + O'Level Points

Method 3 — 75:25 Formula Observed ~2 cycles ago · Not officially confirmed
75% UTME
25% O'Level

This method shifted more weight toward O'Level performance, using a 75:25 split instead of 80:20. It reused the same grading scale as Method 1 (A1=4 … C6=1) for the O'Level component.

UTME Points = (UTME Score ÷ 400) × 75
O'Level Points = (Sum of 5 Grade Points ÷ 20) × 25

Final Aggregate = UTME Points + O'Level Points

📌 Why This Matters

The exact same candidate can land on a noticeably different aggregate score depending only on which formula FUHSI applies in a given year — not because their performance changed. This is why MeritIQ shows all documented methods rather than presenting just one as definitive.

Full Worked Example — All Three Methods Compared

To make the difference concrete, here is one candidate's results run through all three formulas. The candidate has:

  • UTME Score: 233
  • O'Level Grades: Mathematics — B2, English — B3, Physics — B2, Chemistry — C4, Biology — B3

Step 1 — UTME Contribution

1

Method 1 & Method 2 (80% weight)

(233 ÷ 400) × 80 = 46.60

2

Method 3 (75% weight)

(233 ÷ 400) × 75 = 43.69

Step 2 — O'Level Contribution

1

Method 1 & Method 3 grading scale (A1=4 … C6=1)

B2 + B3 + B2 + C4 + B3 = 3.5 + 3 + 3.5 + 2 + 3 = 15 points

Method 1: (15 ÷ 20) × 20 = 15.00

Method 3: (15 ÷ 20) × 25 = 18.75

2

Method 2 grading scale (A1=6 … C6=1)

B2 + B3 + B2 + C4 + B3 = 5 + 4 + 5 + 3 + 4 = 21 points

Method 2: (21 ÷ 30) × 20 = 14.00

Step 3 — Final Aggregates Compared

Method UTME Points O'Level Points Final Aggregate
Method 1 (80:20, old scale) 46.60 15.00 61.60%
Method 2 (80:20, updated scale) 46.60 14.00 60.60%
Method 3 (75:25, old scale) 43.69 18.75 62.44%

The same candidate's aggregate ranged from 60.60% to 62.44% depending purely on which formula was applied — not on any change in their actual JAMB or O'Level performance. This is exactly why understanding the formula matters as much as understanding your own scores.

⚠️ Important Note for Candidates

FUHSI may revise its aggregate formula from one admission cycle to the next, and Method 3 in particular has not been officially confirmed by the university. These methods are presented based on observed historical patterns, not as a guarantee of the formula that will be used in your admission cycle. Always rely on the latest official guidelines published by FUHSI, and treat this breakdown as a tool for understanding the mechanics of aggregate calculation rather than a promise of your exact final score.

It's also worth noting that this breakdown explains how the aggregate itself is computed — it does not tell you what aggregate score is needed to secure admission into a specific course, since cut-off aggregates vary by programme, by how many candidates apply, and by year. For guidance on matching your likely aggregate to realistic course choices, see our companion article, "Best Courses to Choose in FUHSI Based on Your JAMB Score."

Choosing the right course at FUHSI is one of the most important decisions you'll make as an aspirant. Aiming for a programme far beyond your realistic aggregate range can mean missing out entirely, while underselling your own potential can mean settling for less than you could achieve. This guide walks you through a structured way to think about your JAMB score and likely aggregate when choosing a FUHSI programme.

💡 Strategy Note

Your JAMB score is only one part of your final aggregate — your O'Level grades matter just as much, sometimes more, depending on which aggregate formula FUHSI applies that year (see our companion article on how aggregate is calculated). A strong JAMB score with weak O'Level grades, or vice versa, can land in a very different place than either number suggests on its own. Always calculate your full aggregate before deciding on a course, rather than relying on your JAMB score in isolation.

Why Cut-Off Aggregates Aren't Fixed Numbers

Unlike your JAMB score, which is a single fixed number, the aggregate needed to secure a place in a given FUHSI course changes from year to year. It depends on how many candidates apply for that course, how competitive their scores are that cycle, how many spaces FUHSI has available, and which aggregate formula is in effect for that admission cycle. This means any specific cut-off number you see online — including in this article — should be treated as a rough, historical guide rather than a guaranteed threshold for the current cycle.

⚠️ Important Notice

This article intentionally avoids stating fixed numeric cut-offs for individual courses, since presenting outdated or unverified numbers as fact would be misleading. Instead, it focuses on a relative framework — which types of courses tend to be more competitive than others — so you can reason about your own aggregate sensibly. For the most accurate, current information on departmental cut-offs, always check FUHSI's official admission list publications and the JAMB CAPS portal for your specific admission cycle.

A Relative Framework for Course Competitiveness

Rather than memorizing specific numbers that change yearly, it helps to understand the general competitiveness tiers that tend to hold true across most admission cycles at FUHSI and similar health-sciences institutions:

Tier 1 — Highest Competition

Programmes such as Medicine and Surgery and Nursing Science consistently attract the largest number of applicants relative to available spaces nationwide. These courses typically require both a strong UTME score and excellent O'Level grades (mostly A1s and B2s) to be competitive, and even then, admission is not guaranteed given limited capacity.

Tier 2 — High to Moderate Competition

Programmes such as Physiotherapy, Medical Laboratory Science, and Pharmacology-related disciplines tend to be highly sought after but generally have a somewhat larger intake or fewer applicants than Medicine or Nursing, making them realistic targets for candidates with solid (though not necessarily top-tier) UTME scores combined with good O'Level grades.

Tier 3 — Moderate Competition

Programmes such as Biochemistry, Microbiology, and Public Health typically see a more moderate applicant pool. These are often strong options for candidates whose aggregate may not comfortably clear Tier 1 or Tier 2 thresholds but who still have solid, above-minimum performance.

Tier 4 — More Accessible Programmes

Programmes such as Environmental Health Science and other newer or less heavily subscribed health-sciences disciplines tend to have comparatively fewer applicants competing for available spaces, which can make them realistic options for candidates closer to FUHSI's minimum JAMB benchmark — particularly when paired with strong O'Level grades.

A lower JAMB score does not automatically rule out a strong outcome. Because O'Level grades carry significant weight in every documented FUHSI aggregate formula, excellent WAEC/NECO results can meaningfully close the gap with candidates who scored higher in JAMB.
